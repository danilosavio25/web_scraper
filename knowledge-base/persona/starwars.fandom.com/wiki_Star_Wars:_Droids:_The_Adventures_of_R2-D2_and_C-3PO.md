***Star Wars: Droids: The Adventures of R2-D2 and C-3PO*** is an animated television series that features the exploits of the [droids](/wiki/Droid/Legends "Droid/Legends") [C-3PO](/wiki/C-3PO/Legends "C-3PO/Legends") and [R2-D2](/wiki/R2-D2/Legends "R2-D2/Legends") who, over the course of their adventures, often find themselves in the company of new masters—and in new dangerous and difficult situations as a result. The series is set in [15 BBY](/wiki/15_BBY/Legends "15 BBY/Legends")—between the events depicted in [*Star Wars*: Episode III *Revenge of the Sith*](/wiki/Star_Wars:_Episode_III_Revenge_of_the_Sith "Star Wars: Episode III Revenge of the Sith") and [*Star Wars*: Episode IV *A New Hope*](/wiki/Star_Wars:_Episode_IV_A_New_Hope "Star Wars: Episode IV A New Hope").

## Development[]

:   "*I've always been interested in animation. And, again, it's a chance to experiment with ideas and new people and* Star Wars *characters. The* Star Wars *world is much easier to deal with in animation. You can be much more flexible in development of ideas. I've put off doing it for years because I didn't have the time.*"
:   ―George Lucas, in *Starlog* 100[[6]](#cite_note-Starlog_100-6)

*Star Wars* creator [George Lucas](/wiki/George_Lucas "George Lucas") had long been interested in animation, and had wanted to tell *Star Wars* stories in an animated form.[[7]](#cite_note-Starlog_Yearbook_3-7) During production of *[The Star Wars Holiday Special](/wiki/The_Star_Wars_Holiday_Special "The Star Wars Holiday Special")* in 1978, director [David Acomba](/wiki/David_Acomba "David Acomba") showed Lucas a recent film made by [Clive A. Smith](/wiki/Clive_A._Smith "Clive A. Smith")'s animation company [Nelvana](/wiki/Nelvana "Nelvana"). This convinced Lucas to hire the company to produce the animated segment for that film. Lucas was pleased with their work, and in 1984, he again hired them to work on the two animated series he was developing, *Droids* and [*Ewoks*](/wiki/Ewoks_(television_series) "Ewoks (television series)"). These two subjects were chosen because they would appeal to young audiences and because, as the future of the film franchise was uncertain, they would be the least likely characters to conflict with the stories of the feature films.[[1]](#cite_note-A_Star_Wars_CELibration-1) With these two shows, Lucas (who served as executive producer) hoped to raise the standards for Saturday morning animation; he wanted the animation and voice acting to be better than the average shows of the time.[[8]](#cite_note-mikiherman-8) Anthony Daniels felt the same: "I didn't want to do it. I had to be convinced that it was going to be better. I'm no mug—I wouldn't support something *just* because Lucasfilm was doing it."[[9]](#cite_note-Starlog_99-9)

:   "*The challenge on* Droids *was that you essentially had the* Star Wars *story to bring forward without Luke, Leia, Darth Vader and Han Solo.*"
:   ―[Michael Hirsh](/wiki/Michael_Hirsh "Michael Hirsh")[[2]](#cite_note-A_State_of_Nelvana-2)

Pre-production began in May of 1984. During this time Lucas met with the series' producers, directors and writers, who collaborated on story ideas.[[8]](#cite_note-mikiherman-8) The stories were inspired by the science-fiction stories of [Jean Giraud](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Jean_Giraud "wikipedia:Jean Giraud")—specifically [*The Airtight Garage*](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Airtight_Garage "wikipedia:Airtight Garage").[[1]](#cite_note-A_Star_Wars_CELibration-1) The production team tried to convince Lucas to bring in other main characters from the [original trilogy](/wiki/Original_trilogy "Original trilogy"), but he insisted it be focused on the droids.[[2]](#cite_note-A_State_of_Nelvana-2) Lucas laid out his basic ideas for the series, but wasn't involved with day-to-day matters. As the episodes were being worked on, rough cuts were screened for him.[[8]](#cite_note-mikiherman-8) After reading the scripts, Daniels would sometimes rewrite lines of dialogue if he felt they didn't fit his character.[[9]](#cite_note-Starlog_99-9) The series was under strict broadcast standards, and there were limitations—set by ABC's Standards and Practices Board—as to what could be shown or dealt with in the episodes.[[8]](#cite_note-mikiherman-8)[[1]](#cite_note-A_Star_Wars_CELibration-1)[[7]](#cite_note-Starlog_Yearbook_3-7) Writer [Paul Dini](/wiki/Paul_Dini "Paul Dini") commented on this in a 2004 interview: "...we were dealing with a regime at the network that just wanted safe children's programming. Every time we wanted to stretch it a little bit, they would kick up a fuss over it."[[10]](#cite_note-10)

The show's animators tended to specialize in either characters, locales or machinery.[[2]](#cite_note-A_State_of_Nelvana-2) The Korean company [Hanho Heung-Up](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hanho_Heung-Up "wikipedia:Hanho Heung-Up") struggled with the show's designs, which often encompassed up to 24,000 cels per episode. As a result, Clive Smith moved to Korea for eight months in order to assist the company. Smith estimated that each hour-long pair of *Droids* and *Ewoks* episodes cost approximately $500,000 to $600,000 to produce,[[1]](#cite_note-A_Star_Wars_CELibration-1) making them amongst the more expensive animated series for that time.[[7]](#cite_note-Starlog_Yearbook_3-7) The opening theme—"[In Trouble Again](/wiki/In_Trouble_Again "In Trouble Again")"—was performed by [Stewart Copeland](/wiki/Stewart_Copeland "Stewart Copeland") of the band [The Police](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/The_Police "wikipedia:The Police") and written by Copeland and [Derek Holt](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Climax_Blues_Band "wikipedia:Climax Blues Band"),[[8]](#cite_note-mikiherman-8)[[1]](#cite_note-A_Star_Wars_CELibration-1) and the show's [new-wave](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/New_wave_music "wikipedia:New wave music") score was created by [Patricia Cullen](/wiki/Patricia_Cullen "Patricia Cullen"), [David Greene](/wiki/David_Greene "David Greene") and [David Shaw](/wiki/David_Shaw "David Shaw").[[11]](#cite_note-11)

## Release and reception[]

:   "*I get a sense that there is a cresting nostalgia for the old Star Wars cartoon series. Enough time has passed that their faults are more often overlooked and their quaintness idealized.*"
:   ―Abel G. Peña, discussing *Star Wars: Droids* in 2013[[12]](#cite_note-Abel_in_the_Lit_Forum-12)

*Droids* debuted on [September 7](/wiki/September_7 "September 7"), 1985 as part of the *[Ewoks and Droids Adventure Hour](/wiki/Ewoks_and_Droids_Adventure_Hour "Ewoks and Droids Adventure Hour")*. ABC aired a preview special a few days prior entitled *The ABC Saturday Sneak Peek and Fun Fit Test w/ [Tony Danza](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Tony_Danza "wikipedia:Tony Danza"), C-3PO and R2-D2*. (In the special, Olympic gymnast [Mary Lou Retton](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Mary_Lou_Retton "wikipedia:Mary Lou Retton") teaches gymnastics to Danza and the droids.)[[1]](#cite_note-A_Star_Wars_CELibration-1) After airing a full season of 13 episodes, *Droids* was cancelled, due in part to to its high production costs (which were a result the high level of design required relative to other [Saturday morning animation](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Saturday-morning_cartoon "wikipedia:Saturday-morning cartoon") of the time). However, a new one-hour special later aired in 1986,[[2]](#cite_note-A_State_of_Nelvana-2) and the first season was rebroadcast alongside the second season of *Ewoks*.

In the mid 1990s, beginning in 1994, the US [Sci-Fi Channel](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Syfy "wikipedia:Syfy") ran episodes of the series, along with those of its counterpart, *Star Wars: Ewoks*, on its "[Cartoon Quest](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cartoon_Quest "wikipedia:Cartoon Quest")" and "[Animation Station](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/The_Animation_Station "wikipedia:The Animation Station")" blocks of programming.[[2]](#cite_note-A_State_of_Nelvana-2)[[13]](#cite_note-rebelscum-13)[[14]](#cite_note-14)

In [2013](/wiki/2013 "2013"), when *[Star Wars](/wiki/Star_Wars "Star Wars")* author [Abel G. Peña](/wiki/Abel_G._Pe%C3%B1a "Abel G. Peña") wrote the [StarWars.com](/wiki/StarWars.com "StarWars.com") article [The Droids Re-Animated](/wiki/The_Droids_Re-Animated "The Droids Re-Animated"), he mused that after nearly thirty years, fan opinion on *Star Wars: Droids* was likely guided by a nostalgia that idealized its quaint aspects while overlooking any of its faults.[[12]](#cite_note-Abel_in_the_Lit_Forum-12)

## Continuity[]

:   "*The key to a good retcon is to spot the structural weak point of traditional interpretation and then push there and see how far it will bend.*"
:   ―Abel G. Peña, on updating the continuity of *Star Wars: Droids* in 2013[[12]](#cite_note-Abel_in_the_Lit_Forum-12)

*Droids* is set in the nineteen-year period between the rise of the [Galactic Empire](/wiki/Galactic_Empire/Legends "Galactic Empire/Legends") in [*Star Wars*: Episode III *Revenge of the Sith*](/wiki/Star_Wars:_Episode_III_Revenge_of_the_Sith "Star Wars: Episode III Revenge of the Sith"), and the events of [*Star Wars*: Episode IV *A New Hope*](/wiki/Star_Wars:_Episode_IV_A_New_Hope "Star Wars: Episode IV A New Hope").[[2]](#cite_note-A_State_of_Nelvana-2) However, *Revenge of the Sith* introduced a seeming continuity error; at the end of that film, the droids are entrusted to [Raymus Antilles](/wiki/Raymus_Antilles/Legends "Raymus Antilles/Legends"), whom C-3PO refers to in *A New Hope* as "our last master." However, in the *Droids* series, the droids have numerous masters before Captain Antilles. *[Star Wars: The Ultimate Visual Guide](/wiki/Star_Wars:_The_Ultimate_Visual_Guide "Star Wars: The Ultimate Visual Guide")* explains this continuity issue, mentioning that the droids were "accidentally separated" from Antilles "before returning to Captain Antilles' ship, the *Tantive IV*." This explanation was further detailed when [Corla Metonae's](/wiki/Corla_Metonae/Legends "Corla Metonae/Legends") backstory was developed by [Billy Buehler](/wiki/Billy_Buehler "Billy Buehler") (AKA The2ndQuest) for [Hyperspace's](/wiki/Hyperspace:_The_Official_Star_Wars_Fan_Club "Hyperspace: The Official Star Wars Fan Club") *[What's The Story?](/wiki/What%27s_The_Story%3F "What's The Story?")* feature, according to which, she was the person responsible for this separation.[[15]](#cite_note-DB-Metonae,_Corla-15) The circumstances behind the separation were further elaborated on in the StarWars.com blog series The Droids Re-Animated, which specifically cited an unexpected raid on the *Tantive IV* by the pirate group [Lok Revenants](/wiki/Lok_Revenants/Legends "Lok Revenants/Legends") that forced them to abandon the escape pods they had jettisoned during a routine test, which R2-D2 and C-3PO had been inside at the time.

The *Star Wars* [prequel trilogy](/wiki/Prequel_trilogy "Prequel trilogy") contains many [elements](/wiki/List_of_Legends_elements_in_the_films "List of Legends elements in the films") which appear to reference and/or resemble elements from *Droids*.[[2]](#cite_note-A_State_of_Nelvana-2)[[4]](#cite_note-FBtB-4) [*Star Wars*: Episode I *The Phantom Menace*](/wiki/Star_Wars:_Episode_I_The_Phantom_Menace "Star Wars: Episode I The Phantom Menace") shows a [Toong](/wiki/Toong/Legends "Toong/Legends") and mentions [Tund](/wiki/Tund/Legends "Tund/Legends")—the species' second homeworld, both of which first appeared in the novel series [*The Lando Calrissian Adventures*](/wiki/Star_Wars:_The_Lando_Calrissian_Adventures "Star Wars: The Lando Calrissian Adventures"). In "[A Race to the Finish](/wiki/A_Race_to_the_Finish "A Race to the Finish")," the droids end up at a race known as the [Boonta Race](/wiki/Boonta_Speeder_Race "Boonta Speeder Race"). A similar name was used for the [podrace](/wiki/Podracing/Legends "Podracing/Legends") in *The Phantom Menace*, known as the [Boonta Eve Classic](/wiki/Boonta_Eve_Classic/Legends "Boonta Eve Classic/Legends"). The swamp planet of [Bogden](/wiki/Bogden/Legends "Bogden/Legends") is a planet visited by the droids in the series. In [*Star Wars*: Episode II *Attack of the Clones*](/wiki/Star_Wars:_Episode_II_Attack_of_the_Clones "Star Wars: Episode II Attack of the Clones"), [Jango Fett](/wiki/Jango_Fett/Legends "Jango Fett/Legends") says that he was "recruited by a man called [Tyranus](/wiki/Dooku/Legends "Dooku/Legends") on one of the moons of Bogden." [Jann Tosh](/wiki/Jann_Tosh "Jann Tosh")'s [wheel bike](/wiki/48_Roller_wheel_bike/Legends "48 Roller wheel bike/Legends") was [retconned](/wiki/Retroactive_continuity "Retroactive continuity") as a predecessor to [General Grievous's](/wiki/Grievous/Legends "Grievous/Legends") vehicle in *Revenge of the Sith*.[[16]](#cite_note-DB-wheel_bike-16)

The Expanded Universe also incorporated various elements from the series. The [*Shadows of the Empire* soundtrack](/wiki/Shadows_of_the_Empire_(soundtrack) "Shadows of the Empire (soundtrack)") contains liner notes with the lyrics of "[Dha Werda Verda](/wiki/Dha_Werda_Verda "Dha Werda Verda")," written by [Ben Burtt](/wiki/Ben_Burtt "Ben Burtt"). The lyrics contain references to the planet [Roon](/wiki/Roon/Legends "Roon/Legends") from the series. Admiral [Screed](/wiki/Terrinald_Screed/Legends "Terrinald Screed/Legends") appears in [HoloNet News](/wiki/HoloNet_News/Legends "HoloNet News/Legends") and [*Star Wars: Rebellion*](/wiki/Star_Wars:_Rebellion_(video_game) "Star Wars: Rebellion (video game)").

In 2021, [Holocron continuity database](/wiki/Holocron_continuity_database "Holocron continuity database") keeper [Leland Chee](/wiki/Leland_Chee "Leland Chee") clarified that the events in *[The Great Heep](/wiki/The_Great_Heep "The Great Heep")* take place between episodes nine and ten.[[17]](#cite_note-17)

## Adaptations and tie-in media[]

 [![Promotional image for the series](data:image/gif;base64,R0lGODlhAQABAIABAAAAAP///yH5BAEAAAEALAAAAAABAAEAQAICTAEAOw%3D%3D)](https://static.wikia.nocookie.net/starwars/images/e/e3/DroidsStarlog.jpg/revision/latest?cb=20111001180023) [![Promotional image for the series](https://static.wikia.nocookie.net/starwars/images/e/e3/DroidsStarlog.jpg/revision/latest/scale-to-width-down/250?cb=20111001180023)](https://static.wikia.nocookie.net/starwars/images/e/e3/DroidsStarlog.jpg/revision/latest?cb=20111001180023)

In 1985, [Kenner](/wiki/Kenner_Products "Kenner Products") produced a toy line based on the series, including [action figures](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Action_figure "wikipedia:Action figure"), ship models, and other items. [Random House](/wiki/Random_House "Random House") also published a series of children's books based on various episodes of the series. An [LP record](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/LP_record "wikipedia:LP record") and a [cassette](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Compact_Cassette "wikipedia:Compact Cassette") featuring music from the show were released in France.[[18]](#cite_note-SWArchive-Droids_et_Ewoks_de_Marchandises-18) In 1986, [Marvel Comics](/wiki/Marvel_Comics "Marvel Comics")' [Star Comics](/wiki/Star_Comics "Star Comics") imprint published a *Droids* comic book, which was based on the cartoon series. The comic also had the name [*Star Wars: Droids*](/wiki/Star_Wars:_Droids_(1986) "Star Wars: Droids (1986)"). The comic series takes place between [10](/wiki/10_BBY/Legends "10 BBY/Legends")-[6 BBY](/wiki/6_BBY/Legends "6 BBY/Legends"), unlike the TV series which was placed around [15 BBY](/wiki/15_BBY/Legends "15 BBY/Legends"). The bimonthly series ran for a year, ending with issue #8. Significant issues include #4, which crossed over with the [*Ewoks*](/wiki/Star_Wars:_Ewoks_(1985) "Star Wars: Ewoks (1985)") comics series, and #6–8, whose story was titled "Star Wars According to the Droids," retelling *A New Hope* with new scenes told from the perspective of the droids. The series was drawn by comic legend [John Romita Sr.](/wiki/John_Romita_Sr. "John Romita Sr.") [Dark Horse Comics](/wiki/Dark_Horse_Comics "Dark Horse Comics") also ran a couple of *Droids* mini-series in 1994 and 1995. A [*Droids* computer game](/wiki/Star_Wars:_Droids_(video_game) "Star Wars: Droids (video game)") was released in [1988](/wiki/1988 "1988") for the [ZX Spectrum](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/ZX_Spectrum "wikipedia:ZX Spectrum") and [Amstrad CPC](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Amstrad_CPC "wikipedia:Amstrad CPC") home computers.

After the series finished production, Nelvana sent the majority of pre-production materials to Lucasfilm, who later partnered with the former company Royal Animated Art to sell these materials to private owners. Some were bought by a California business,[[2]](#cite_note-A_State_of_Nelvana-2) and some were later given out as part of the [Hyperspace fan club](/wiki/Hyperspace:_The_Official_Star_Wars_Fan_Club "Hyperspace: The Official Star Wars Fan Club").[[19]](#cite_note-SWArchive-Early_Star_Wars_Animation_Collectibles-19) In [2007](/wiki/2007 "2007"), [Gentle Giant](/wiki/Gentle_Giant_Studios "Gentle Giant Studios") released an animated maquette of Boba Fett based on Fett's appearance in the series. It was a Celebration exclusive; of the 1000 produced, 700 were sold at [Celebration IV](/wiki/Celebration_IV "Celebration IV"), while the remaining 300 were sold at [Celebration Europe](/wiki/Celebration_Europe_(2007) "Celebration Europe (2007)").[[20]](#cite_note-SWArchive-Classic_Animation_Boba_Fett_Maquette_a_Celebration_Exclusive-20) Also at Celebration IV, *Droids* merchandise was auctioned off from the Lucas Licensing archives.[[21]](#cite_note-SWArchive-Collector_Panels_and_More_at_Celebration_IV-21)[[22]](#cite_note-SWArchive-Celebration_IV_Charity_Auction-22)[[23]](#cite_note-SWArchive-Auctioning_off_the_Galaxy..._For_a_Great_Cause!-23) In 2010, StarWars.com celebrated the 25th anniversary of both *Droids* and *Ewoks* by featuring a series of articles about the collectibles available from each series.[[24]](#cite_note-SWArchive-25_Years_of_Collecting_Droids_and_Ewoks!-24) In 2015, Gentle Giant re-released several *Droids* figures as 12" convention exclusives.[[25]](#cite_note-SW-Calling_All_Galactic_Treasure_Hunters:_SDCC_Star_Wars_Collectibles_Sneak_Peek-25) In 2021, Hasbro released several new figures for their vintage collection and Black Series that were based on *Droids*.[[26]](#cite_note-Habbro_2021-26)

### Home video[]

The series received its first home video release in England and Germany in 1988 through [CBS/Fox Video](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/CBS/Fox_Video "wikipedia:CBS/Fox Video") and featured twelve of the episodes, omitting episode nine, "Coby and the Starhunters." In 1990, J2 Communications released *The Star Wars Trilogy Animated Collection*. The collection consisted of three VHS tapes; one tape contained one episode, while the other two tapes contained two episodes each—with one of those repeating the episode from the single-episode tape. Each tape began with a *Star Wars Animated Classics* trailer promoting the "Special Double Length Edition" volumes. However, for the single-volume tapes, the white box covers were shown, but differing content was advertised.[[2]](#cite_note-A_State_of_Nelvana-2)[[13]](#cite_note-rebelscum-13) All thirteen episodes including *The Great Heep* were released on Region 1 VHS in Mexico through CBS/FOX.

On June 26, 2002, [prequel trilogy](/wiki/Prequel_trilogy "Prequel trilogy") producer [Rick McCallum](/wiki/Rick_McCallum "Rick McCallum") responded to a question about a complete DVD release on StarWars.com's now defunct "Ask the Jedi Council" feature, in which he said, "I hope so. Definitely. At some point after we're finished with Episode III, we'd really like to make all of that material available to our fans on DVD. Unfortunately, we won't be even thinking of making any firm plans until we're finished with this trilogy."[[27]](#cite_note-SWArchive-Ask_the_Lucasfilm_Jedi_Council:_Ewoks_on_DVD?-27) He also expressed this privately to [Paul Ens](/wiki/Paul_Ens "Paul Ens").[[28]](#cite_note-28) According to [Pablo Hidalgo](/wiki/Pablo_Hidalgo "Pablo Hidalgo"), a full series release was considered as an alternative to the [*Animated Adventures*](/wiki/Star_Wars_Animated_Adventures:_Droids "Star Wars Animated Adventures: Droids") DVDs: "I don't know what numbers are run in the mysterious background that decides to say 'You know what? That doesn't look like a product that we can move.'"[[29]](#cite_note-29) At [2005](/wiki/2005 "2005")'s [Celebration III](/wiki/Celebration_III "Celebration III"), McCallum and Lucasfilm's Vice President of marketing [Jim Ward](/wiki/Jim_Ward_(Lucasfilm) "Jim Ward (Lucasfilm)") dodged questions about a complete DVD release of the series.[[30]](#cite_note-30)

At the [Celebration VI](/wiki/Celebration_VI "Celebration VI") "StarWars.com and Beyond" panel, it was mentioned that both *Droids* and *Ewoks* may be streamed on StarWars.com in the future.[[31]](#cite_note-31) In April [2021](/wiki/2021 "2021"), StarWars.com announced that the series would be released on [Disney+](/wiki/Disney%2B "Disney+") later that year.[[32]](#cite_note-Vintage-32) It finally appeared on the streaming service on [June 18](/wiki/June_18 "June 18"), 2021 as part of the [*Star Wars* Vintage Collection](/wiki/Star_Wars_Vintage_Collection "Star Wars Vintage Collection").[[33]](#cite_note-Fantha-33)
